# Product

## Register

product

## Users

PAIO contestants, alumni, coaches, organisers, national teams, educators, and competitive programming enthusiasts use the site to explore official results and understand performance across editions. They often arrive with a specific question: who competed, how a contestant or country performed, which tasks were difficult, or who has the strongest historical medal record.

## Product Purpose

PAIO Stats preserves the competition's public record in one connected, trustworthy interface. It turns official edition data into browsable scoreboards, country histories, contestant profiles, task statistics, and all-time rankings. Success means a visitor can answer a factual question quickly, follow links to supporting detail, and understand how every aggregate was calculated.

## Brand Personality

Clear, credible, celebratory. The voice should feel like a careful official record with enough warmth to honor contestants and the growing Pan-African informatics community.

## Anti-references

- A sports-betting dashboard that sensationalizes rankings or turns every value into a promotional graphic.
- A generic SaaS analytics page filled with decorative metric cards, gradients, and empty visual effects.
- A dense legacy database whose abbreviations, ranking rules, and horizontal tables are unexplained.

## Design Principles

1. Lead with the record. Put names, results, and relationships ahead of decoration.
2. Explain the ranking. A visitor should understand what a number means and how ties are handled.
3. Preserve context. Link aggregates back to contestants, countries, editions, and tasks.
4. Celebrate without distorting. Medal color and hierarchy may add ceremony, but never obscure accuracy.
5. Grow by aggregation. New editions should flow into historical views without one-off page logic.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.2 AA. Tables, status, and medals must remain understandable without color alone; interactive controls need keyboard focus and explicit labels; layouts must work on small screens and with enlarged text; and motion must respect reduced-motion preferences.
